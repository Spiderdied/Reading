🐬 Dolphins

One of the ocean's most intelligent and social creatures — dolphins have fascinated humans for thousands of years.

Summary

Dolphins have long been seen as friendly, helpful creatures. Ancient stories describe them rescuing drowning sailors. As scientists learn more, they discover that dolphin society is surprisingly complex — showing care, communication, and community in ways that resemble human behaviour.

Social Behaviour

Dolphins do not simply swim in groups — they form genuine communities. They look after sick members, support pregnant mothers, and protect weaker individuals. This level of care is similar to what we see in human families and is one reason scientists believe dolphins are highly intelligent mammals.

Some researchers have proposed that dolphins have a language, though it is more accurate to say they communicate through sounds, movements, and signals rather than using words in the way humans do.

The question of whether dolphins are more intelligent than humans is controversial. The most commonly cited argument for human superiority — that we can kill them more easily than they can kill us — is the weakest possible evidence of intelligence. In fact, the author of the passage argues the opposite: the more we learn about these animals, the more our actions against them reveal our own limitations rather than theirs.

Dolphins have been observed teaching their young, working cooperatively when hunting, and even grieving for lost members of their group — behaviours that were once thought to be uniquely human.

Key Terms

MammalA warm-blooded animal that breathes air and nurses its young with milk.
CommunicationSharing information using sounds, signals, or movement.
Social structureThe organised relationships within a group or community.
SuperiorityThe quality of being greater or more advanced than others.

🧠 The Effects of Stress

Stress is more than just feeling overwhelmed — it has real, measurable effects on the body, emotions, and long-term mental health.

Summary

The English expression "Stop the world, I want to get off!" captures the feeling of being overwhelmed by stress. Stress — defined as pressure or tension — is one of the most widespread causes of health problems in modern life. It affects us physically, emotionally, and mentally.

Physical Effects

When the body is under stress, several systems are affected at once. The heart may beat faster, miss beats, or face increased blood pressure. The respiratory system can develop problems such as asthma or abnormally fast breathing, which depletes the body's carbon dioxide. The digestive system is also vulnerable — stress commonly causes stomach aches and disrupted digestion.

Emotional and Mental Effects

Stress does not stay in the body — it quickly spills into emotions. People under stress often feel anxious, fatigued, or prone to sudden anger. A usually calm parent, for example, might shout at a child over something minor simply because workplace stress has eroded their patience.

Over the long term, unmanaged stress can develop into serious mental health conditions, including depression, addiction (often as an attempt to escape the pressure), and eating disorders such as anorexia.

Depression involves an intense, prolonged feeling of sadness and hopelessness that goes far beyond ordinary low moods. It is one of the most common outcomes of chronic stress. Alcoholism and other addictions frequently develop when a person uses substances to temporarily escape stressful feelings — but this only worsens the underlying problem over time.

The key message is prevention: recognising stress early and taking deliberate steps to reduce it — through rest, social support, or professional help — can protect both physical and mental wellbeing.

Key Terms

StressPressure or tension that affects the body and mind.
AnxietyA feeling of worry, nervousness, or unease.
DepressionA serious mental illness involving deep sadness and hopelessness.
AddictionAn unhealthy dependence on a substance or behaviour.

📱 Digital Habits Across Generations

From grandparents joining Facebook to teenagers sleeping beside their phones — how different generations interact with technology tells us a great deal about modern life.

Summary

Today's older adults are adopting social media in growing numbers, while younger teenagers are quietly moving away from platforms like Facebook. But all generations remain closely attached to their smartphones — in very different ways and for very different reasons.

The Older Generation

In the United Kingdom, adults over 55 are joining Facebook in increasing numbers, making them one of the platform's largest user groups. For many, the motivation is staying connected with family — especially grandchildren. Sheila, aged 59, describes Facebook as far superior to waiting for letters or photographs to arrive in the post. It allows her to feel genuinely present in her grandchildren's lives. Ironically, the grandchildren themselves are leaving Facebook behind.

Teenagers and Smartphones

Young people under 17 are spending less time on Facebook but remain deeply attached to their phones. Fifteen-year-old Chloe keeps her phone beside her at night, checks it before sleeping, and picks it up the moment she wakes. Researchers note that this constant phone use is reducing the time teenagers spend with friends in the real world — a significant social cost.

Peter, a 38-year-old father, represents the middle generation — those who were early adopters of smartphones. He admits he was once constantly connected, feeling as though he was always working. Realising that he could not tell his children to reduce screen time while he himself was permanently on his phone, he made a change: in the evenings and at weekends he removes his SIM card and uses a basic mobile phone that only makes calls and sends texts. He retains connection for emergencies but sets a healthier example for his children.

The article ends with a question worth sitting with: will the trend toward a less digital life eventually spread across all generations?

Key Terms

Social mediaPlatforms like Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter for sharing content online.
Early adoptersPeople who start using new technology before most others do.
AddictionAn unhealthy reliance on something — in this case, digital devices.
IronicallyIn an unexpected, sometimes amusing way — the opposite of what you'd expect.

🚶 A Long Walk Home

A story about a teenage lie, a father's silent response, and the most unforgettable lesson either of them ever shared.

Summary

Sixteen-year-old Jason is given the privilege of driving his father to a distant village. He promises to collect him at 4pm, but instead spends hours at the cinema and returns two hours late. He invents a lie about the car needing repairs — but his father has already called the garage and knows the truth. What follows is one of the most powerful lessons of Jason's life.

The Story — Retold in Simple Language

Jason's father trusted him with the car — a rare privilege for a new driver. When Jason lost track of time at the cinema, his first instinct was to hide the truth. He invented a story about mechanical repairs. But the moment he met his father's eyes, something felt wrong.

His father told him quietly: "I'm disappointed that you felt you had to lie to me." He had already called the garage. He already knew everything. Rather than shouting, he said something more painful — that if his son felt the need to lie to him, it meant he had failed as a father. Then he turned and began to walk the 18 miles home.

Jason followed slowly behind in the car, pleading, apologising, watching his father walk alone through the dark. For every one of those 18 miles, Jason felt the full weight of what he had done. He never lied to his father again.

Themes and Lessons

The story explores honesty, trust, and parental love. Jason's father does not punish through anger — he punishes through painful, silent action. His walk is a statement: a father who has been let down does not rage; he reflects and endures. The lesson lands far deeper than any reprimand could have.

The father's choice to walk instead of riding home also carries an element of self-examination. He says he is "angry with himself," not with Jason — because he believes a parent is responsible for the character of the child they raise. This reflects a deep and mature understanding of the parent-child relationship.

From a language learning perspective, the story introduces past perfect tense beautifully — "he had called," "I had gone," "we had planned." These constructions help show the sequence of events when two things happen in the past, with one completing before the other began.

Key Terms

RemoteFar away from other places; difficult to reach.
ImmersedDeeply absorbed or involved in something.
ConfessTo admit the truth about something you did wrong.
ContemplateTo think deeply and seriously about something.
RelentTo give up a firm decision and agree to something.

🦟 Mosquitoes

Tiny, persistent, and surprisingly sophisticated — mosquitoes are among the most studied insects on the planet. Here's why.

Summary

There are roughly 3,500 species of mosquitoes worldwide. The word "mosquito" comes from Portuguese and means "little fly." Despite their small size, mosquitoes are difficult to swat because of specialised wing scales that reduce friction and allow them to move quickly and efficiently through the air. Their wings beat approximately 600 times per second, producing the familiar buzzing sound.

The Life Cycle

Mosquitoes pass through four distinct stages: egg, larva, pupa, and adult. All of these stages depend heavily on water. Eggs are laid in or near water — some species prefer still water in containers, others prefer marshes or swamps. Once hatched, larvae (called "wigglers" for their jerky movements) feed beneath the water's surface but must rise regularly for air. After shedding their skin four times, they become pupae, which also breathe air and continue feeding. Adult mosquitoes emerge after 10–14 days in most species, though warmer temperatures can shorten this significantly.

Once adult mosquitoes emerge, they mate within a few days. Both males and females feed on nectar and sugar sources. However, only females bite humans and animals — they require a blood meal to develop their eggs. After feeding, a female rests for two to three days before laying her eggs, and this cycle continues for roughly one to two weeks, which is the full adult lifespan of a mosquito.

Controlling mosquito populations therefore means targeting their breeding grounds — particularly any standing water. Removing old tyres, emptying buckets, and clearing blocked drains can significantly reduce local mosquito numbers.

How Do They Find You?

Mosquitoes are attracted by warmth, movement, and carbon dioxide — all of which humans produce constantly. When you laugh, talk, or breathe, you release carbon dioxide that draws mosquitoes toward you. So the social activities of conversation and laughter are, ironically, exactly what attracts them — not your personality, but your biology.

Key Terms

LarvaThe second life stage of a mosquito — an immature, worm-like form.
PupaThe third stage, between larva and adult, where transformation occurs.
FrictionResistance when two surfaces move against each other.
OrganicDerived from living things — such as leaves, grass, and soil.
MaturityThe state of being fully developed — adulthood.
