Dolphins

Exploring the intelligence, social behavior, and remarkable nature of one of the ocean's most fascinating creatures.

🌊 Reputation & History

Dolphins have long been considered the friendliest creatures in the sea. Stories of dolphins rescuing or helping drowning sailors have been told since Roman times, showing that this reputation goes back thousands of years.

🤝 Complex Social Behavior

The more scientists study dolphins, the more they discover how socially sophisticated these animals truly are. Dolphins look after sick members of their group, care for pregnant mothers, and protect the weakest individuals — behaviors very similar to what humans do in their own communities.

💬 Communication

Some scientists have proposed that dolphins possess their own language. However, most experts believe it is more accurate to say that dolphins communicate with one another without relying on words as we know them. Their communication system is complex but fundamentally different from human language.

🧠 Intelligence

The reading raises a bold and thought-provoking question: could dolphins be more intelligent than humans? The author challenges the common argument that humans are superior because we can kill dolphins more easily than they can kill us — pointing out that this is actually a very poor measure of intelligence or worth.

⚖️ The Author's Main Message

The writer argues that the more we learn about dolphins, the less superior we appear when we choose to harm or destroy them. Killing a creature does not prove our superiority — if anything, it may demonstrate the opposite. The passage ultimately calls on readers to respect these remarkable mammals.

Dolphins are mammals, not fish — they breathe air, give birth to live young, and nurse their babies.
The author uses irony: the "proof" of human superiority (our ability to kill them) actually makes us look worse, not better.
Dolphins' social structures — caring for the sick, the elderly, and the unborn — mirror some of humanity's most valued traits.

Reading 2

Psychological Pressure

Understanding how stress affects the body, the emotions, and the mind — and why it is one of modern life's biggest health challenges.

😤 What Is Stress?

The reading opens with a well-known English expression — "Stop the world, I want to get off!" — which captures the feeling of panic and overwhelming pressure that stress creates. Stress is defined simply as pressure or tension. It is one of the most widespread causes of health problems in modern life, affecting people physically, emotionally, and mentally.

❤️ Physical Effects

Stress has a wide range of physical consequences. It can affect the heart by increasing the pulse rate, causing missed heartbeats, and raising blood pressure. It can impact the respiratory system, leading to asthma or abnormally fast breathing which reduces carbon dioxide. It also disrupts digestion, causing stomach aches and difficulty processing food properly.

😟 Emotional Effects

People under stress often feel anxious, tired, and irritable. They may experience panic attacks. Stress tends to make people overreact disproportionately to small, everyday problems. A parent who is normally calm and gentle might shout at a child over something trivial — like spilling juice — simply because the pressure of work has made them emotionally fragile.

🏥 Long-term Mental Effects

If stress continues over a long period without treatment, it can lead to serious mental illness. Depression — an extreme and prolonged feeling of sadness and hopelessness — can result from chronic stress. People may also turn to alcohol or drugs as a way of coping, leading to addiction. Eating disorders such as anorexia are sometimes caused by stress and are almost always made worse by it.

💡 The Author's Conclusion

The writer concludes by summarizing the three-layered danger of stress: it attacks the body, disrupts the emotions, and — if left untreated — eventually threatens mental health. The final advice is clear and even a little playful: reduce stress by "stopping the world" and resting for a while. The message is that managing stress is not a luxury — it is essential for overall well-being.

Stress affects the heart, lungs, and stomach — three of the body's most vital systems.
Alcoholism and eating disorders like anorexia can both be results of long-term unmanaged stress.
The article title "Stop the world, I want to get off!" is a metaphor for feeling overwhelmed by life's pace.

Reading 3

Generation & Technology

How different generations use social media and smartphones — and what happens when digital habits reshape real-life relationships.

👴 Grandparents Online

Surprisingly, it is the older generation — those aged 55 and above — who are joining social media platforms like Facebook in rapidly growing numbers. In the UK, there are 3.5 million Facebook users aged 55–64 and 2.9 million over the age of 65. Sheila, aged 59, explains that she joined to watch videos and photos of her grandchildren shared by her daughter — a much richer connection than the old days of waiting for letters and photos by post.

👶 Teenagers Leaving Facebook

Ironically, while grandparents are joining Facebook, teenagers are leaving it. Only 2.2 million UK users are under 17. However, young people are not disconnecting from technology — they are simply moving to other apps and devices. Fifteen-year-old Chloe even sleeps with her phone, using it as her alarm clock, and checks it the moment she wakes up and just before she falls asleep.

🤝 Real Life vs. Screen Life

The contrast between generations is striking. Teenagers are spending so much time on their phones at home that they are missing out on face-to-face time with friends. Meanwhile, Sheila has reconnected with old school friends she had not spoken to in forty years, using Facebook to organize in-person meetings across the country. For her, social media has entirely transformed her social life for the better.

👨‍👧 Parents: The Early Adopters

Peter, a 38-year-old father of two teenagers, represents the "middle generation" — the early adopters of smartphones who paved the way for their children's addiction. He admits he used to be constantly connected, always feeling like he was working. Concerned about setting a poor example, he now removes his SIM card on evenings and weekends, putting it into an old basic phone that can only call and send texts.

🔮 The Bigger Question

The article ends with a thought-provoking question: will the generation above and below Peter — grandparents and teenagers — eventually follow his lead toward a less digital lifestyle? The piece challenges all readers to think about whether technology is genuinely enriching their relationships or quietly replacing them.

The over-55s will soon be Facebook's second biggest user group in the UK.
"Ironically" is a key word — grandparents use the platform their grandchildren have abandoned.
Peter's strategy: keeping one basic phone for emergencies shows it is possible to reduce screen time without being completely disconnected.
